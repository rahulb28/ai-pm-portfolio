Overview

What is "walking the store"?

Retail merchandising has a practice called "walking the store" — a manager physically moves through the store as a customer would, looking for what's broken, what's confusing, and what's missing. It's one of the most effective forms of product discovery because it grounds decisions in the actual experience rather than aggregated metrics.

Digital product teams rarely do this systematically. Manual journey audits are slow, hard to standardize, and nearly impossible to run across the full matrix of personas, devices, and funnel stages. As a result, real friction in the live experience often goes undetected until it shows up in conversion data weeks later.

I built Walk the Store to bring that same discipline to paid acquisition funnels — using AI to simulate, evaluate, and synthesize real customer journeys at a scale no manual process could match.

Context: This was built as a side project while working on a high-traffic consumer travel marketplace with a large paid acquisition funnel. The patterns apply to any product where users arrive through paid channels and flow through a multi-step conversion experience.
The problem

Why manual audits don't scale

Product teams rely on a mix of dashboards, session recordings, support tickets, and periodic user research to understand their funnel. These sources are valuable, but they share a common limitation: they're all retrospective. By the time a funnel issue surfaces in conversion data, it's often been live for weeks.

Manual audits can catch issues proactively, but they have real constraints:

🐢
Speed
A thorough end-to-end funnel review across two devices, three personas, and five entry points takes 2–3 days of focused PM time.
🎲
Consistency
Different reviewers notice different things. Without a structured framework, findings vary significantly by the auditor's background and current priorities.
📐
Coverage
Real users arrive with different intents, contexts, and devices. Manual reviews rarely cover the full matrix — they tend to collapse to a single "happy path."
🔗
Linkage to action
Even when issues are identified, they often sit in documents rather than feeding directly into prioritized experiment hypotheses.
What I built

An AI-assisted audit workflow

Walk the Store is an AI-assisted workflow that simulates real customer arrivals, evaluates the journey through persona-specific lenses, and produces structured findings mapped to product opportunities.

The key design decision was to combine deterministic rule-based checks with AI-assisted synthesis — rather than using AI for everything. Some things (price consistency, funnel continuity, element presence) are better evaluated with explicit rules. AI is most valuable at the synthesis layer: clustering observations, inferring intent mismatches, and generating testable hypotheses.

How it worked

System design and workflow

The workflow runs in six stages. The first three are largely automated; the last three are where AI does the heavy lifting.

01
Journey setup — intake query and entry point
The audit begins with a structured intake: search query or intent signal, entry channel (e.g., paid search, display, email), target device (desktop or mobile), and any specific funnel stage to focus on. This scopes the audit to a realistic user scenario rather than an abstract walk-through.
02
Persona selection — evaluating through a specific user lens
Each audit is run through a defined persona with explicit needs and decision signals. Examples: a last-minute leisure traveler (urgency-sensitive, price-anchored, trust-seeking), a flexible business traveler (needs itinerary options, values time savings), or a deal-seeking family planner (filters-heavy, comparison-driven). The persona shapes what counts as a "gap" at each funnel stage.
03
Funnel walkthrough — surface-by-surface observation
Browser automation simulates the full journey: acquisition touchpoint → landing page → search results → detail view → checkout entry. At each surface, the system captures the full rendered state on both desktop and mobile viewports. Deterministic checks run first: price consistency across acquisition and landing, funnel continuity (does the query carry through?), element presence (is the key CTA above the fold on mobile?). These don't need AI — they have clear pass/fail criteria.
04
Issue detection — AI-assisted pattern recognition
After deterministic checks, AI evaluates the experience against the active persona's needs. The prompt includes: the persona definition, the funnel stage, and the observed page state. AI is asked to identify: trust gaps (does this page keep the promise made at acquisition?), conversion friction (are decision signals visible, accessible, and clearly prioritized?), mobile-specific issues (hidden filters, interaction cost, scroll depth requirements), and message mismatch (does the tone and framing match what a [persona] arriving from [channel] would expect?). AI is explicitly instructed not to flag issues that pass deterministic checks — this prevents hallucinated findings.
05
Severity scoring — structured prioritization
Each finding is scored on three dimensions: reach (what % of users hit this funnel stage?), impact (estimated effect on conversion or trust, rated 1–5), and reversibility (how quickly can an experiment test a fix?). The composite score drives prioritization. High-reach + high-impact + fast-to-test = top of the backlog. This isn't AI judgment — it's a deterministic scoring formula applied to AI-generated findings.
06
Opportunity synthesis — from observations to hypotheses
The final AI pass clusters related findings into themes, generates testable product hypotheses in a standard format (We believe [change] will [outcome] for [persona] because [rationale]), and maps each hypothesis to a likely experiment type (A/B test, multivariate, holdout). The output is formatted to drop directly into a product backlog or experiment planning doc — not a narrative report.
Example findings

What the system surfaced

Below are representative findings from audit runs, generalized and anonymized. Each includes the severity, the observed issue, and the generated experiment hypothesis.

High
Acquisition promise not reflected on landing page
Users arriving from a paid ad emphasizing "flexible options" landed on a page with no visible flexibility messaging. The promise made at the acquisition touchpoint was not carried through to the landing experience, creating an immediate trust discontinuity for flexibility-seeking personas.
💡 Hypothesis: Surfacing flexibility messaging above the fold on the landing page will improve conversion for users arriving from flexibility-oriented acquisition copy.
High
Price inconsistency between acquisition touchpoint and landing experience
The price displayed in acquisition creative did not match the first price shown on the landing page (due to fee disclosure timing and tax display differences). For price-sensitive personas, this gap created a trust risk that could cause early funnel abandonment before users understood the reason for the difference.
💡 Hypothesis: Aligning the price anchor shown at acquisition with the initial price shown on landing (using the same fee-inclusive or fee-exclusive format consistently) will reduce early bounce for price-sensitive segments.
Medium
Key filters not discoverable on mobile without extra interaction
On mobile, the most-used decision filters (flexibility, price range, key attributes) were hidden behind a collapsed panel requiring an additional tap to reveal. For task-focused users, this added interaction cost reduced the likelihood of filter engagement, leading to lower-quality result sets and reduced conversion.
💡 Hypothesis: Surfacing the top 2–3 filters as persistent chips at the top of the mobile results page will increase filter engagement and downstream conversion for mobile-first users.
Medium
Urgency and trust signals absent for last-minute shoppers
Users with last-minute intent (same-day or next-day searches) were shown the same results layout as users planning weeks out. Urgency cues (limited availability, time-sensitive pricing, social proof) were absent or inconsistently applied. For this persona, the generic results experience failed to accelerate the decision.
💡 Hypothesis: Introducing urgency-aware result ranking and inline availability signals for short-lead queries will increase conversion rate for last-minute intent segments.
Low
Business-oriented decision signals buried below the fold
For business traveler personas, key decision signals (cancellation policy, flexibility tier, loyalty value) were positioned low in the results card hierarchy, requiring scroll to reach. While the information was present, its placement reduced salience for time-constrained evaluators who scan rather than read.
💡 Hypothesis: Elevating flexibility and policy signals to the top 3 visible attributes in results cards for business-segment audiences will improve click-through to detail pages.
Product impact

What this changed about discovery

The value of this system wasn't just finding individual issues — it was changing the quality and speed of the entire product discovery process.

Dimension Before After
Audit speed 2–3 days for a single persona × device pass Full matrix (3 personas × 2 devices) in under 2 hours
Coverage consistency Varies by auditor focus and time available Structured checklist applied identically on every run
Finding format Freeform notes in docs or slide decks Structured findings with severity scores and experiment hypotheses
Backlog linkage Manual translation from audit notes to tickets Hypotheses formatted for direct intake into experiment backlog
Repeatability Ad hoc, triggered by incidents or stakeholder requests Can be scheduled or triggered on demand after any release
Why this matters

AI as a force multiplier for PM judgment

This project is a concrete example of where AI genuinely augments product work — not by replacing PM judgment, but by scaling the parts of discovery that are currently bottlenecked by human time and consistency.

The most important design decision was knowing what not to use AI for. Deterministic checks (price consistency, element presence, funnel continuity) are more reliable as rules. AI is reserved for the synthesis tasks it's actually good at: recognizing patterns across observations, applying persona reasoning, and generating structured hypotheses.

The output is designed to feed a process, not to be the process. A PM still decides which hypotheses are worth testing, how to prioritize, and what success looks like. What changes is how much raw material they have to work with, and how quickly they can get it.

PM takeaway

The broader lesson

"
AI is most useful in product work when it scales judgment — especially in discovery, auditing, and opportunity framing. The goal isn't to automate the PM. It's to make sure the PM is never the bottleneck on finding the next right thing to test.
— PM takeaway from Walk the Store

For hiring managers evaluating AI PM candidates: the question worth asking isn't "can they use AI tools?" — it's "do they know where AI adds value and where it doesn't?" This project was built to demonstrate exactly that distinction.
